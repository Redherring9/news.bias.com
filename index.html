Joss Stone to perform free concert in Florianópolis in May to celebrate 100 years of the Hercílio Luz Bridge

Joss Stone faz show gratuito em Florianópolis em maio para celebrar 100 anos da Ponte Hercílio Luz

British singer Joss Stone will headline a free concert in Florianópolis, Brazil, this May as part of celebrations marking the centennial of the Hercílio Luz Bridge, one of Santa Catarina's most iconic landmarks. The performance on May 16th will take place on the Continental waterfront, accompanied by a fireworks and light show, drawing both music lovers and those eager to honor a beloved piece of local history. The bridge itself holds a special place in the region's heart. Opened in 1926, it was the first structure to connect Santa Catarina Island to the mainland, transforming the city's landscape and daily life. Over the past century, it has carried streetcars, horse-drawn vehicles, pedestrians, bicycles, and motor traffic—witnessing nearly every mode of transportation the modern era has offered. Today, it handles around 16,000 vehicles and thousands of pedestrians each day, a testament to its enduring role in the community. Affectionately known as the "Old Lady," the Hercílio Luz Bridge has evolved from a simple crossing into a gathering place, a tourist attraction, and an engineering landmark. This celebration feels like more than just a birthday party for infrastructure—it's a recognition of how a bridge can stitch together past and future, serving as both a practical link and a symbol of connection across generations.

Fishers deploy 'ninja tactics' to outsmart fish-stealing seals

In the Coorong lagoons of South Australia, commercial fishers are locked in an escalating contest with long-nosed fur seals that have learned to raid their catches. Tracy Hill, who runs a family fishing business with her husband, describes a practice they call "ninja fishing"—a hurried routine of deploying nets, hoping for a quick catch, and retrieving them before seals arrive to tear man-sized holes in the gear and steal the fish. Even when fishers stay close to their nets, seals work the far end, shredding the fine nylon and making off with the haul. The situation has grown particularly difficult this year following a six-month algal bloom and the recent departure of mullet on a breeding migration, leaving fishers with leaner catches and fewer options. Seal numbers in the area have been climbing, with recent counts showing an increase from a mean of 90 in 2024 to 108 in 2025, peaking during winter months. The South Australian government has been monitoring the population since 2015, and a research project examining the economic and ecological impacts of the seals is nearing completion. Meanwhile, fishers have been left with limited tools: noise-making "crackers" meant to scare the animals have proven largely ineffective, and the seals have grown savvy enough to follow boat lights at night. This story offers a candid glimpse into the daily frustrations of small-scale fishing, where wildlife protection and livelihood clash in unexpected ways. It's a reminder that coexistence is rarely simple, and that even the most resourceful "ninja tactics" can't always outwit nature's opportunists.

Country Life: Dragon fruit a sweet choice for storm-scarred Northland?

In the orchards of Kerikeri, Northland, researchers are cultivating an unexpected crop: dragon fruit. While the exotic pink-red fruit with its distinctive scaly skin is typically associated with tropical climates, scientists have been pleasantly surprised by how well smaller, sweeter varieties are flourishing under protective covers in New Zealand's changing conditions. The trial, led by the Bioeconomy Science Institute, represents a decade-long collaboration with Vietnam—one of the world's leading dragon fruit producers—to develop more flavoursome, disease-resistant cultivars with better shelf life. The plants, members of the cactus family, grow on specially designed wire systems, with some protected by plastic tunnels that shield them from cold winters and increasingly intense rainfall. While tropical dragon fruit typically produce five harvests annually within three years, the New Zealand varieties take four years to reach full production with two annual harvests. Researchers hope the tunnel systems will boost yields and add a third harvest while improving fruit quality and making harvesting easier. The initiative is part of a broader exploration by Northland Inc into unusual crops like papaya, pineapple, and turmeric, driven by climate change and the region's need for agricultural diversification. This story offers a glimpse into how farmers are proactively adapting to environmental shifts rather than simply reacting to them. It's a reminder that climate change, while challenging, can also open unexpected agricultural possibilities—and that the future contents of school lunchboxes might look quite different from what we're used to seeing today.

'Derek' the dingo a concern for some as he roams streets in outback town

A lone dingo has become an unexpected regular in the suburban streets of Alice Springs, Australia, charming some residents while prompting safety concerns from others. The animal, affectionately named Derek by locals, has been visiting backyards and even dog parks for over a year, developing what former mayor Fran Kilgariff describes as a playful friendship with her domesticated dogs. Derek plays chase, lounges on verandahs, and has become familiar enough that the household dogs greet him warmly—though Kilgariff is careful never to approach him directly, fully aware he remains a wild animal. The Alice Springs Town Council has received multiple reports about Derek's wanderings and is monitoring the situation closely. Ranger manager Luke Allen emphasizes that while no incidents have occurred, the unpredictability of a wild dingo in residential areas poses potential risks. Complicating matters, dingoes are protected in the Northern Territory, and there are strict regulations against feeding or approaching them. Allen suspects someone may be feeding Derek, which could compromise his ability to survive in the wild and may ultimately necessitate relocation for his own welfare. This story offers a gentle window into the complicated relationship between humans and wildlife in outback communities, where the boundaries between wild and domestic blur in unexpected ways. It's a reminder that coexistence requires both appreciation and caution—Derek may be charming, but he's still fundamentally wild, and finding the balance between protecting both community safety and a protected species presents a uniquely Australian dilemma worth pondering.

Rehab center opens for Brazil’s golden-headed lion tamarins amid urban sprawl threat

Brazil has opened its first rehabilitation center dedicated to golden-headed lion tamarins, small endangered monkeys caught in an increasingly perilous squeeze between shrinking forests and expanding cities. The facility, inaugurated at the State University of Santa Cruz in Bahia state, addresses a growing crisis: tamarins venturing into urban areas where they're electrocuted on power lines, struck by vehicles, or attacked by domestic dogs. Some have even been spotted eating fruit inside supermarkets—a seemingly charming sight that masks a troubling reality. Biologist Leonardo Oliveira, who has studied these monkeys for over two decades, emphasizes that humans are encroaching on tamarin territory, not the other way around. The species exists nowhere else on Earth, and its situation has grown dire. Over the past three decades, their range has shrunk by 42 percent, and their population has plummeted nearly 60 percent to fewer than 25,000 individuals. Much of their remaining habitat consists of cacao agroforestry farms, where the monkeys thrive among native tree canopies and feast on their favorite fruit. But even these refuges are disappearing as farms convert to soy monocultures and cattle pasture. The new center can initially house three tamarin groups, with plans to expand to eight, providing veterinary care and preparing rescued animals for release in safer forest areas. There's a poignant symmetry to the center's timing: it opened on March 26, a day Ilhéus now celebrates both its official mascot—the golden-headed lion tamarin—and Brazil's national cacao day, honoring the deep connection between the species and the crop that sustains them both. This story matters because it shows communities recognizing their responsibility to species pushed to the brink, and taking tangible steps toward coexistence.

New Evidence from Ethiopia: How Did Humans Live 100,000 Years Ago?

أدلة جديدة من إثيوبيا: كيف عاش البشر قبل 100 ألف عام؟

Archaeologists working in Ethiopia's Afar region have uncovered fresh insights into how early humans lived around 100,000 years ago. The study, published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, analyzed the Halibēy site in the Awash Valley—one of the world's most significant locations for understanding human origins. Among the discoveries: over 3,000 stone tools, partial remains of three early Homo sapiens individuals, and traces of fire and animal bones preserved in ancient floodplain sediments. What makes these findings particularly intriguing is the picture they paint of early human adaptability. Rather than inhabiting only open, dry savannas as previously thought, these communities navigated a complex, shifting landscape of grasslands, riverside forests, and seasonal floods. Lead researcher Jonas Bēni notes that this mixed environment offered crucial advantages—water, shade, raw materials for tools—suggesting humans weren't confined to a single habitat but thrived in dynamic, resource-rich settings. The tools, crafted from local volcanic rock, appear to have been made and used during temporary stays rather than permanent settlement, hinting at a mobile lifestyle tied to seasonal availability of resources. Not everything is conclusive: charred tree stumps and burnt soil could indicate controlled use of fire, but researchers remain cautious, as natural wildfires can't be ruled out. Similarly, while thousands of animal bones were found, there's little evidence of butchery marks, leaving open questions about hunting practices. This story matters because it quietly reshapes our understanding of early human resilience and resourcefulness, revealing ancestors who were not simply surviving in harsh conditions but skillfully navigating a complex, ever-changing world.
