The site they toured was a stark portrait of modern agriculture—lifeless fields devoid of insects, butterflies scarce, and not a single person encountered during their walk. This "ruined landscape," as one guest described it, represented typical industrial farming: absentee ownership, contract labor, and soil worked solely for wheat and beans. Burrell's company Nattergal purchased the farm for £13.8 million with a plan that would shock traditionalists: abandon 6,000 years of farming, stop all crops and chemicals, even destroy the drainage systems farmers had labored to install. This story matters because it tests whether rewilding can scale beyond Knepp's well-documented success. With his pragmatic farmer's demeanor and radical vision, Burrell is asking whether reversing environmental collapse can become not just inspiring, but replicable—whether healing the land might also prove economically viable across Britain's depleted countryside. The question hanging in that June air was simple yet profound: can nature return if we simply step back and let it?

Matariki: How Māori navigators use the stars to keep their waka on course

In an age of GPS and digital navigation, Māori voyagers are keeping alive an ancient practice that relies on stars, ocean currents, and an intimate relationship with the natural world. Hinerapa Rupuha, who captained the double-hulled canoe Ngāhiraka Mai Tawhiti on its 2025 journey from Samoa to Tonga, is among a new generation learning to navigate vast stretches of the Pacific using techniques passed down through oral tradition—no technology, no instruments, not even a watch. The practice requires memorizing at least 67 key stars, along with their positions, movements, and relationships to one another. Navigators learn that one star, Mintaka in Orion's belt, rises precisely due east at zero degrees, providing a natural compass from which all other directions can be determined. But knowledge alone isn't enough. Rupuha describes the process as building a relationship with the stars—understanding how they shift depending on your location and learning to read the sky as a living map. Even if a star like Matariki is obscured, a navigator can locate it by finding its neighbor, Taumata-kuku. Manihera Forbes, who mentors emerging navigators, explains that voyagers use the width of their hand as a measuring tool against the horizon, creating a mental "star compass" layered over what they see. This knowledge traces back to Mau Piailug, a Micronesian master navigator who famously told his students to trust memory over paper, which can blow away or get wet. The revival of these traditions represents more than navigation—it's a testament to the resilience of indigenous knowledge systems and the profound human capacity to read the world without technological mediation. In learning to see the ocean through the eyes of their ancestors, these navigators are charting a course not just across water, but across time.

What sets these toys apart isn't just their charm, but the science hidden inside: they use UV light to transform liquid substances into solid mini foods, like jiggly gelatin or golden-brown muffins. The timing has to do with Earth's tilt and population distribution. Interestingly, this peak occurs after the June solstice, not on it. As the sun shifts southward following the solstice, it reduces daylight in sparsely populated far northern regions but extends it into densely populated equatorial areas like Indonesia and the Philippines, bringing about 10 million more people into the light. Hidden in northern Tocantins, Brazil, the Serra da Cangalha preserves evidence of a cataclysmic event from 300 million years ago. A meteorite weighing approximately 20,000 tons created what is now the country's fourth-largest impact crater—measuring 13.7 kilometers across. The most intriguing detail: the meteorite never actually touched the ground. Instead, it disintegrated mid-air, releasing such colossal energy that it deformed the ancient sedimentary rocks below and carved concentric rings into the landscape, still visible from satellite images today like ripples spreading across water. Today, it offers a rare opportunity for geotourism: visitors can hike three-kilometer trails through the impact rings, explore waterfalls and the newly discovered "Meteor Cavern," or take paramotor flights for aerial views of the massive structure. The researchers 'flying' and getting barrels on the reef for science

For four decades, researchers have been tracking the health of the Great Barrier Reef using an unusual and exhilarating technique called "manta towing" — being pulled through the water while clinging to a rope, frantically counting fish and coral every two minutes. Dr. Daniela Ceccarelli describes the experience as underwater flying, occasionally catching a wave barrel in rough weather while recording observations on waterproof paper. She's completed around 3,000 of these tows in just five years, sometimes accompanied by dolphins or whales, and once witnessed the unlikely spectacle of an octopus punching a fish. This distinctive counting method forms the backbone of the Australian Institute of Marine Science's monitoring program, which has now amassed 40 years of continuous data from 490 different reef locations. The dataset is considered the gold standard for coral reef management, combining these broad-scale manta tows with detailed scuba surveys. The publicly accessible database allows anyone to explore the health of specific reef sections, answering the perennial question of how the Great Barrier Reef is faring with transparency and detail. What makes this story quietly remarkable is how consistency in method has created something irreplaceable. By keeping their approach unchanged over decades, researchers have built what's likely the world's longest continuous coral reef monitoring record. This constancy has allowed them to assess the real-world impact of controversial conservation policies, like the 2004 no-fishing zones, and document both devastation and resilience, including coral's surprising recovery after the 2016-17 bleaching event. It's a reminder that patient, unglamorous scientific work can create the evidence needed to understand and protect fragile ecosystems.

Meet Mr Row Row, the man who invented Norway's viral World Cup Viking row

Ole Frøystad, a blond-haired Norwegian football fan who never takes off his Viking horns, has inadvertently created one of the most joyful spectacles of this World Cup. His "Viking row" chant — a synchronized routine where supporters sit, mime rowing an imaginary oar in time with drum beats, and shout "ro" (Norwegian for "row") — has swept through stadiums, city squares, and subway cars across the tournament host cities in the United States. The inspiration came years earlier while Frøystad watched Norwegian club Rosenborg, whose fans divided the team's name into syllables during chants. He loved the sharp impact of "ro" and tucked the idea away. When Norway qualified for its first World Cup since 1998, he realized the word's meaning — rowing — could anchor a chant that honored Viking heritage while creating a powerful stadium atmosphere. Working with supporter group Oljeberget, they refined the choreography and added the drum beat that drives the rhythm. The result has been electric: coordinated rows in Times Square, spontaneous performances on escalators and flights, and even players joining in after victories. Star striker Erling Haaland called it "bigger than football." What makes this story quietly remarkable is how a simple idea from a passionate fan from a tiny mountain island has unlocked something playful and communal in a nation often described as reserved. It's a reminder that sports fandom at its best isn't just about watching — it's about creating moments of shared joy that transcend the game itself.

The Guardian view on gene-edited humans: darker uses must be acknowledged alongside medical ones | Editorial

Gene-editing technology has advanced rapidly since Crispr-Cas9 emerged in the early 2010s, and new research suggests that genetically altered humans may be closer than many realize. While 70 countries currently ban human germline editing—genetic changes that could be passed to future generations—recent studies using more precise base-editing tools on embryos are pushing the boundaries of what's possible. Two new studies demonstrate how this technology could potentially eradicate hereditary diseases, though researchers acknowledge it's not yet safe for clinical use. The legal landscape, however, may be less solid than it appears: most prohibitions rest on safety concerns rather than fundamental ethical objections, meaning each technological improvement weakens the case for maintaining bans. Public opinion appears surprisingly receptive to these developments. New polling across several European countries shows majority support for using gene editing to eliminate life-threatening conditions like cystic fibrosis, with substantial support even for addressing manageable conditions like asthma. This represents a dramatic shift from the strong opposition that greeted IVF research in the 1980s. Yet this acceptance brings complications. Evidence already exists of couples traveling abroad to screen embryos for desirable traits, and collaborations between IVF companies and base-editing labs in the United States suggest the step from medical treatment to designer babies may be smaller than expected. This story matters because it captures a pivotal moment when hypothetical ethical debates are becoming practical policy questions. As safety arguments gradually lose their force with each technological advance, societies will need to grapple with deeper questions about what genetic changes should be permitted and how to prevent misuse—challenges that regulation can limit but likely never fully eliminate.

British swallowtail split from European cousins much earlier than thought, study finds

A new genetic study has revealed that Britain's endangered swallowtail butterfly is far more ancient and distinctive than previously believed. The British swallowtail, found only in the Norfolk Broads wetlands, was long thought to have developed its unique characteristics—smaller size, darker coloring—during the 8,000 years since rising seas isolated it from continental populations. Instead, whole-genome sequencing shows it split from European cousins between 200,000 and 1.7 million years ago, making it a wetland specialist that likely once ranged across northern Europe's marshes. This discovery arrives at a crucial moment for conservation strategy. Some experts had proposed introducing the more common continental swallowtail to Britain, potentially allowing it to hybridize with the rarer British subspecies. The continental variety thrives on multiple food plants, while British swallowtail caterpillars depend almost exclusively on milk parsley, a rare wetland plant. Both butterfly and plant now face twin threats: rising sea levels are salinating the freshwater Norfolk Broads, while warming temperatures are already bringing continental swallowtails to southern England during warm summers. Most British breeding populations live at or below sea level, making them especially vulnerable. What makes this story quietly remarkable is how it reframes conservation priorities through the lens of deep history. Rather than viewing the British swallowtail as a recent, fragile variant, scientists now recognize it as a unique evolutionary lineage and the last remnant of a once-widespread wetland population. Conservationists are now planning to establish new populations in protected wetlands across England, from Suffolk to Somerset to Yorkshire—preserving not just a rare butterfly, but a living connection to Europe's ancient wetland past.

Okinawa’s seaweed at a crossroads as harvesters and scientists race to save a fading tradition

On the tidal flats of Okinawa, harvesters wade through shallow water collecting mozuku, a slippery brown seaweed that has sustained island traditions for generations. Served in vinegar, soups, and noodle dishes, mozuku is more than a local delicacy—it accounts for over 99% of Japan's production and supports both an important aquaculture industry and a growing health food sector thanks to its unique fucoidan content. Yet this familiar rhythm of harvest and tide now faces an uncertain future. Rising ocean temperatures and repeated coral bleaching events are disrupting the patterns harvesters once relied upon. Production has swung wildly in recent years, plunging from over 22,000 tons in 2007 to just 8,000 tons three years later following unusually warm El Niño conditions. One harvester notes that seasonal predictions, once reliable, have become difficult, leaving communities anxious about what lies ahead. In response, scientists are turning to genomics to understand the seaweed's biology and resilience, while fisheries cooperatives explore reef restoration as a way to protect the coastal ecosystems that mozuku depends upon. This story captures a quiet struggle playing out across Okinawa's coastline, where tradition meets climate uncertainty. It's a reminder that even the most humble ingredients carry entire ecosystems and economies within them, and that preserving what seems everyday often requires innovation, patience, and care. The fate of mozuku is also the fate of a culture shaped by the sea.

What makes this story quietly remarkable is how a few scraps of fabric became a lasting symbol of personal freedom. Today, women of all ages and body types wear bikinis with the same confidence, resisting magazine pressure for the "perfect beach body." The bikini remains what it was at its debut: not just swimwear, but a statement — one that has endured for eight decades and shows no signs of fading.

‘They ate the shrimp, they even ate the crab’: Thai fishers count the cost of a voracious invader

A voracious fish from west Africa is upending Thailand's aquaculture industry and reshaping coastal ecosystems. The blackchin tilapia, first spotted spreading in Thailand in 2011, has proven nearly impossible to stop, infiltrating at least 19 provinces from Bangkok's canals to the tourist beaches of Pattaya. For shrimp farmers like Wallop Khunjaen, the invasion has been devastating—in just two months, the fish devoured nearly all of the million baby shrimp in his pond, forcing him to abandon farming altogether. Native species, including fiddler crabs, have grown scarce or disappeared entirely from local waters. Thai authorities have tried various control measures: introducing predator fish, developing sterile blackchin tilapia, and paying bounties for catches. Thousands of tonnes have been removed, yet experts agree eradication is no longer possible. The fish reproduces rapidly, adapts to both fresh and brackish water, and quickly rebounds after population controls. Some restaurateurs, like Adisorn Jamsuksaward, are experimenting with serving the fish—and report it's delicious—though cultural hesitation remains. Researchers now suggest the most practical path forward is finding economic uses for the invader, whether as animal feed or human food. This story offers a window into a growing global challenge: invasive fish species linked to aquaculture and the pet trade. It's a reminder that ecological disruptions rarely have simple solutions, and that communities on the front lines must balance survival with adaptation, turning an unwelcome guest into something they can live with—perhaps even eat.

Didi's ceramic expertise proved essential when the gold requirements seemed overwhelming, as she developed alternative techniques using gold glaze and specialty tiles. The finished mosaic measures 180 by 180 centimeters and currently hangs at the Maleny Yoga Shed, though its weight demands a reinforced wall. The couple hopes to share it with the community before selling it to fund a community art studio and their next collaborative project.
