How eletrofunk 'dribbled past' sertanejo prejudice and invaded parties across Brazil's interior

Como eletrofunk 'driblou' preconceito do sertanejo e invadiu festas pelo interior do país

A hybrid music genre called eletrofunk is sweeping through Brazil's rural heartland, bridging an unlikely cultural divide. The style blends the percussive tamborzão rhythms and vocals of funk carioca with the pulsing beats of electronic house music, creating something fans describe as "funk but not funk." What began as a niche sound in the periphery has become a mainstay at street parties and rural festivals across the country's interior, particularly in the Center-West states. The genre's rise wasn't without resistance. For years, traditionalist sertanejo (Brazilian country music) artists and audiences viewed eletrofunk with skepticism, dismissing its lyrics and questioning its place at rodeos and agricultural fairs. That began shifting around 2020 when established sertanejo stars like Ana Castela and Luan Pereira collaborated with eletrofunk artists, lending mainstream credibility to the sound. Post-pandemic, major events like the prestigious Barretos Rodeo Festival have welcomed eletrofunk DJs like Jiraya UAI onto lineups alongside country music legends, drawing younger crowds while maintaining ties to rural roots. What makes this story quietly remarkable is how eletrofunk found its audience through decidedly analog means in a digital age. Artists distribute their music via USB drives rather than relying solely on streaming platforms—a practical solution for the "som automotivo" (car sound system) culture prevalent in rural Brazil, where modified trucks and cars become mobile dance floors. These drives work offline and deliver better sound quality for the massive speaker systems that dominate street parties. It's a reminder that musical movements don't always follow predictable paths, and that cultural acceptance can happen when communities recognize shared roots beneath unfamiliar sounds.

Water vole volunteers 'get very excited by flattened poo'

Along a chalk stream in Hampshire, England, volunteers are finding reasons to celebrate — in the form of flattened droppings. These telltale signs indicate that water voles, once abundant along British waterways, are breeding successfully after a decade-long reintroduction effort. The volunteers wade through the River Meon searching not just for stamped-down poo, which marks territorial claims, but also for precisely angled plant cuttings and small food caches that reveal the presence of these industrious rodents. The water vole's story is one of dramatic decline and cautious hope. Since the 1970s, an estimated 90 percent of Britain's water voles have disappeared, largely due to predation by non-native American mink and habitat loss, earning them the distinction of being the country's fastest-declining mammal. But the Meon Valley Partnership has been working to reverse this trend since 2013, releasing more than 2,800 water voles into the river. Regular monitoring now shows they're thriving, with no mink detected in the area. The voles' return brings wider benefits too: their constant nibbling of over 200 plant species keeps riverbanks healthy, while their burrows improve soil quality and create micro-ecosystems. This story offers a quiet reminder that patient conservation work can yield tangible results. It's a tale told through the enthusiasm of volunteers who've learned to read a landscape through the smallest clues — a testament to both the fragility of wildlife and the possibility of recovery when communities commit to restoration.

Antarctica's first dinosaur fossil found in drawer after 40 years

Sometimes the most remarkable discoveries happen not in the field, but in the quiet corners of museum storage. A bone collected from Antarctica's James Ross Island in 1985 sat unidentified in a drawer for nearly four decades before being confirmed as the continent's first discovered dinosaur fossil. Geologist Dr. Mike Thomson had recorded it in his field notes as a "vertebra of large reptile," sketching the 10-centimeter bone with characteristic scientific precision before sending it to the British Antarctic Survey's collection in England. The fossil's true identity came to light when collections manager Dr. Mark Evans, trained in vertebrate paleontology, recognized its distinctive shape as belonging to a titanosaur—the largest land-dwelling dinosaurs, some reaching over 35 meters in length. This particular bone likely came from a relatively modest specimen, perhaps 6 or 7 meters long, living in an Antarctica unrecognizable from today's frozen landscape. About 82 million years ago, the continent was warm, heavily forested, and temperate. Scientists believe this dinosaur floated out to sea after death, eventually settling and fossilizing on the seabed. Beyond validating Dr. Thomson's careful fieldwork from 40 years prior, the discovery offers clues about how titanosaurs dispersed across Earth's southern continents. With no titanosaur fossils yet found in Australia and only limited evidence in New Zealand, this Antarctic specimen suggests these giants traveled between landmasses that were once connected. As ice continues to retreat, researchers suspect more dinosaur evidence may emerge from beneath Antarctica's frozen archive, rewriting our understanding of prehistoric life at the bottom of the world—one patient discovery at a time.

Aquatic animal and terrestrial meat trades now almost on par, FAO report finds

A new United Nations report has revealed a milestone in global food systems: the trade value of aquatic animals—fish, crustaceans, and mollusks—has nearly caught up with that of beef, pork, and chicken combined. The FAO's biennial State of World Fisheries and Aquaculture report, released in June, shows that farmed aquatic animal production surpassed 100 million metric tons annually for the first time in 2024, contributing to a record total of 235 million metric tons when wild-caught fish and algae are included. This shift reflects the rapid growth of aquaculture, which FAO officials say is reaching production levels that traditional fishing never achieved. With the global population heading toward 10 billion, aquatic foods play an increasingly vital role: more than 40% of people worldwide rely on them for at least a fifth of their protein. Yet access remains uneven—Africans consume an average of just 9 kilograms per person annually, compared to 26 kilograms in Asia. Meanwhile, the report found that sustainably managed fish stocks dropped to a new low of 62.4%, covering about 70% of tracked fisheries. This story matters because it captures a quiet transformation in how humanity feeds itself. As aquaculture expands to meet growing demand, the report underscores the need for thoughtful governance and innovation to ensure this growth is both sustainable and equitable. It's a reminder that our relationship with the ocean is evolving rapidly—and the choices made now will shape food security and marine ecosystems for generations to come.

Hong Kong’s urban cockatoos could be a genetic lifeline for Indonesian ancestors

In an unexpected twist on conservation, a flock of roughly 200 yellow-crested cockatoos living amid Hong Kong's skyscrapers and traffic may represent a lifeline for their critically endangered relatives in Indonesia. These urban parrots, descendants of escaped pets, make up about 10% of the species' global population—a remarkable statistic given that fewer than 2,000 individuals remain in their native Indonesian and Timor-Leste habitats, threatened by habitat loss and wildlife trade. What makes Hong Kong's population particularly valuable is its genetic diversity. Researchers conducting genomic analysis discovered that these city-dwelling birds carry genetic signatures from all four recognized subspecies of yellow-crested cockatoos. More than half belong to a lineage from Lombok, Indonesia, where the species is now believed to be locally extinct. Rather than being genetic bottlenecks, the urban cockatoos have maintained diversity comparable to wild parrot populations, making them a potential "Biodiversity Ark" that could support future genetic rescue efforts in Indonesia. Yet this avian sanctuary faces its own crisis: typhoons and tree pruning have eliminated over 60% of the natural nesting sites the cockatoos previously used. Researchers are now partnering with local schools and parks to install artificial nest boxes with monitoring cameras, ensuring this globally significant population can continue breeding. The story offers a gentle reminder that conservation sometimes appears in unlikely places, and that urban populations—often dismissed as ecologically irrelevant—may hold surprising value for species survival, provided we recognize and protect them.

São Tomé declares first two of eight planned marine protected areas

The small island nation of São Tomé and Príncipe has taken a significant step in ocean conservation by officially designating its first two marine protected areas along its coast. The West African nation announced the protections at the Our Ocean Conference in Kenya, marking progress on an ambitious plan to establish eight marine protected areas covering 93 square kilometers in the Gulf of Guinea. The newly protected zones safeguard critical habitats including mangroves, nesting beaches, and rocky reefs that support threatened marine turtles, seabirds, and diverse ocean life. The two designated areas—Santana on the eastern coast and Ilhéu das Rolas–Malanza–Jalé on the southern coast—employ a mixed approach to protection. Within their boundaries, fully protected no-take zones prohibit all industrial fishing and resource extraction, while other sections remain open to artisanal fishers using traditional, legal methods. The remaining six protected areas, all around Príncipe Island, have received government approval and await final presidential authorization. Implementing these protections presents real challenges, including limited state resources, enforcement difficulties, and the need to balance conservation with fishing communities' livelihoods. What makes this effort quietly remarkable is its commitment to avoiding "paper parks"—protected areas that exist only on maps. São Tomé and Príncipe is centering local communities, fishers, and civil society organizations in co-management arrangements, recognizing that lasting ocean protection requires both ecological knowledge and community buy-in. For a small nation taking on the complex work of marine governance, this story offers a thoughtful example of conservation that acknowledges both the vulnerability of ocean ecosystems and the people who depend on them.

Telling one guiña from another: It’s all about the angle

In the temperate forests of Chile and Argentina lives the guiña, a small wildcat so subtly marked that researchers have long struggled to tell one individual from another. This identification challenge has left conservationists with a critical blind spot: without knowing how many guiña exist in threatened populations, it's nearly impossible to protect them effectively. Now, a simple shift in perspective—literally—may offer a solution. Researchers led by Nicholas Galvez at the Pontifical Catholic University of Chile repositioned their camera traps to point downward from tree branches, capturing images of the cats from above rather than at ground level. This "zenith" angle revealed what side views could not: the unique constellation of black spots on each guiña's back, allowing the team to identify twelve individual cats in Chile's Reñihué Valley. The technique addresses a longstanding problem in studying these cryptic felines, which were only recently downlisted from vulnerable to least concern by the IUCN—a decision that sparked debate given that several subpopulations remain under serious threat from habitat loss, persecution, and forest fires. While the method shows promise, it's not without complications. More than half the images captured were unusable, either because they featured melanistic black-furred individuals whose spots are invisible, or because image quality was too poor. Still, conservation scientists see the approach as potentially transformative for filling crucial data gaps, not just for guiña but possibly for other elusive small cat species. In a field where even the simplest question—how many are there?—can be maddeningly difficult to answer, sometimes all it takes is looking at the problem from a different angle.

Why Ukraine is rewilding in the heat of war

In southwestern Ukraine, amid the sounds of distant explosions and missile strikes, an unusual conservation project is quietly restoring an ancient landscape. Rewilding Ukraine has been reintroducing native species to 600 hectares of the Tarutino Steppe since 2017, bringing back horses, deer, water buffalo, and their star attraction: 35 kulan, or wild donkeys, whose distinctive brays now echo across grasslands that were once silent. Local volunteer Petro Hramatik monitors these animals as they transform the ecosystem through natural grazing. The kulan reduce dry vegetation that might otherwise fuel the wildfires ignited by land mines and summer heat, while their droppings enrich soil and improve water retention. The Tarutino Steppe was once a thriving temperate grassland supported by fertile 'chernozem' soils, but centuries of intensive farming converted it to cropland. After Soviet-era agriculture collapsed in the 1990s, the abandoned landscape became fire-prone and degraded. Now, the return of ancient grazers alongside smaller mammals like marmots is restoring soil fertility and carbon sequestration, essentially healing the land while war rages nearby. This story is worth attention because it reveals how hope and practical action can coexist with crisis. Why Indian mangoes remain rare in New Zealand supermarkets

Mel Sunquist, field biologist and mentor to generations of conservationists

Mel Sunquist, who died in May at age 85, helped transform the study of wild carnivores by pioneering the use of radio telemetry to track tigers, jaguars, and other elusive animals. In the 1970s, when camera traps and GPS didn't yet exist, Sunquist ventured into Nepal's Royal Chitwan National Park to collar and follow tigers through challenging terrain. His groundbreaking research produced some of the first detailed evidence of tiger movements, territories, and social behavior, replacing guesswork with careful observation and giving conservation efforts a scientific foundation. Born in Minnesota in 1941, Sunquist spent much of his career at the University of Florida, where he taught wildlife ecology from 1987 to 2014 and mentored generations of biologists who went on to lead conservation programs worldwide. Colleagues and students remember him as humble, patient, and deeply respectful of the animals he studied. One story stands out: after a tiger he'd darted for visiting officials drowned in a rain-filled road before Sunquist could reach him, the loss stayed with him for decades. The memory shaped his approach, reinforcing his belief that each animal deserved care and consideration, not just as data but as an individual. This story matters because it reminds us that behind every scientific breakthrough are people who carry the weight of their work with them. Sunquist's legacy isn't just the knowledge he uncovered about wild cats, but the ethical framework and gentle rigor he passed on to those who followed—a commitment to sound science, thoughtful mentorship, and treating animals with the respect they deserve.
