Tongan scholars reawaken ancient musical tradition

A bamboo nose flute once used to wake Tongan royalty—and famously played beneath Queen Elizabeth II's window during her 1953 visit—is being brought back to life by three Tongan scholars in New Zealand. The fangufangu, a traditional instrument with deep spiritual and historical significance, nearly vanished under colonial and missionary influence. Now, an exhibition at Porirua's Pātaka Museum is helping reconnect the Tongan diaspora with this distinctive art form. The exhibition brings together the work of Dr Adriana Māhanga Lear, Dr Rachael Hall, and Dr Sione Faletau, who are collaborating with three fangufangu from the 1800s held in Te Papa's Pacific collection. Dr Lear emphasizes the instrument's connection to Hikuleʻo, a powerful goddess in Tongan cosmology, and to Pulotu, the ancestral homelands. Dr Faletau is exploring the intersection of ancient and modern by translating the flute's sounds into algorithms and digital patterns, finding surprising resonances between the instrument's natural origins and contemporary technology. Many Tongans have heard recordings of the fangufangu but have never encountered the physical instrument, making this revival both a cultural and sensory reconnection. This story offers a glimpse into how communities reclaim traditions that were nearly erased, using both reverence and innovation. The fangufangu revival is more than nostalgia—it's about excavating knowledge from colonial archives, placing it in conversation with living memory, and ensuring younger generations inherit something remarkable that was almost lost. It's a quiet act of cultural resilience, turning whispers from the past into something the present can hear again.

A 17th Century 'supercomputer' once owned by Indian royalty sells for record in auction

A magnificent 17th-century brass astrolabe, once owned by Indian royalty, has sold for more than £2 million at a Sotheby's auction in London, setting a record for astronomical instruments from the Islamic world. Described as perhaps the largest astrolabe in existence, this hand-held "supercomputer" was part of the royal collection of Maharaja Sawai Man Singh II of Jaipur before passing to his wife, the glamorous Maharani Gayatri Devi, and eventually into private hands. The extraordinary instrument was crafted in early 17th-century Lahore by brothers Qa'im Muhammad and Muhammad Muqim for Aqa Afzal, a Mughal nobleman who administered the city. Weighing over eight kilograms and measuring nearly 30 centimeters in diameter—roughly four times the size of typical astrolabes from that era—it showcases the pinnacle of the renowned "Lahore School" of astrolabe-making. The device contains 94 cities marked with their coordinates, 38 star pointers with names in both Persian and Sanskrit, and plates calibrated with remarkable precision. Astrolabes themselves are marvels of ancient technology, two-dimensional projections of the three-dimensional universe that could calculate time, measure distances, map stars, and even cast horoscopes—essentially the smartphones of their day. This story resonates because it bridges science, art, and cultural exchange in a single object. The blend of Persian and Sanskrit inscriptions reveals the cross-cultural sophistication of the Mughal court, where technical mastery met aesthetic ambition. It's a tangible reminder that centuries before modern computing, craftspeople were creating instruments of breathtaking complexity and beauty—tools that compressed the cosmos into something you could hold in your hands.

Caring for Grandchildren Is Good for the Brain

Cuidar dos netos faz bem ao cérebro

A growing body of research suggests that caring for grandchildren offers cognitive benefits for older adults, adding scientific weight to what many grandparents already feel intuitively. A recent study published in Psychology and Aging found that even modest involvement in grandchild care can support memory and overall brain function in people over 50. Researchers followed nearly 2,900 grandparents with an average age of 67 over six years as part of the English Longitudinal Study of Ageing. They discovered that grandparents who provided any level of care scored higher on memory and verbal fluency tests than those who didn't, regardless of age or health conditions. Interestingly, the type of engagement mattered more than the amount of time spent: mentally active tasks like helping with homework or playing games showed the strongest associations with cognitive performance. The study also revealed a notable gender difference—grandmothers who provided care experienced slower cognitive decline over time, while grandfathers enjoyed cognitive benefits without the same protective effect against decline. Researchers suggest this may relate to grandmothers typically taking on more hands-on, structured responsibilities. The benefits flow both ways. Previous research from Oxford and Boston College has shown that children with strong grandparent connections tend to have fewer behavioral and emotional difficulties, and grandparents often serve as sources of resilience during family crises. This story quietly underscores an often-overlooked truth: the intergenerational bonds within families aren't just emotionally valuable—they may be cognitively and psychologically protective for everyone involved.

RSPB cautiously welcomes slight increase in UK nightingale population

The nightingale, one of Britain's most celebrated songbirds, has seen a modest uptick in population after decades of precipitous decline. Known for their complex, energetic songs that fill the dawn chorus after their arrival from West Africa, nightingales have dropped 90% in number since the 1970s and now occupy only the south and east of England. But recent surveys show an 8.9% increase in singing males over the past decade, with RSPB reserves recording their second-highest totals in more than ten years. At RSPB Northward Hill in Kent, home to Britain's largest nightingale population with 47 singing males, the reasons for recovery become clear. Former arable land has been transformed into dense woodland and scrub through careful planting and natural regeneration, creating ideal habitat for these famously secretive birds. Coppicing and the cultivation of low-growing vegetation have been crucial, providing the thick cover nightingales need for nesting and foraging. Yet challenges remain: habitat degradation from reduced coppicing and rising deer populations, declining insect prey, climate change, and threats from proposed housing developments near key sanctuaries all loom large. As migratory birds wintering in a small region around the Gambia, British nightingales face vulnerabilities conservationists can only partially address. This story matters because it offers a rare glimmer of hope in an era of biodiversity loss, while reminding us how fragile that progress remains. The nightingale's fate serves as a barometer for the health of Britain's woodland ecosystems, making each singing male not just a source of legendary beauty, but a quiet measure of whether conservation efforts can turn the tide.

All-female voyage to tackle plastics problem sets sail

An international crew of ten women has set sail from Auckland aboard a 70-foot ketch, embarking on a research voyage to investigate plastic pollution in New Zealand and Pacific waters. Led by expedition leader Rowan Henthorn, the team will travel to Great Barrier Island, the Bay of Islands, and Tonga as part of a global initiative by UK-based nonprofit eXXpedition—one of ten such missions planned over the next 18 months. The crew is using sophisticated equipment to trace ocean plastics back to their land-based sources. This voyage represents a thoughtful approach to an overwhelming problem. These magnificent creatures undertake the longest annual migration of any mammal, traveling up to 20,000 kilometers round trip between the Arctic and Baja California. Now, researchers are witnessing them feed, mate, and interact in the bay's waters. But alongside the wonder comes growing alarm: many of these whales are dangerously thin, and record numbers are dying. In 2024, twenty-one gray whales perished in San Francisco Bay—an unprecedented toll. Seven more have died so far in 2025. Researchers believe declining food sources in the Arctic may be driving hungry whales to seek alternative feeding grounds. Yet starvation isn't necessarily the direct cause of death. A recent study published in Frontiers in Marine Science found that vessel strikes are killing nearly one-fifth of the gray whales entering the bay, with malnourished animals particularly vulnerable to collisions. This story matters because these whales may be messengers, carrying warnings about larger changes happening beneath the ocean's surface. The bay offers scientists a rare window into how climate change is reshaping migration routes and food availability.
