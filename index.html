Rare fossil found in Brazil reveals new species of snake that lived in São Paulo interior millions of years ago

Fóssil raro achado no Brasil revela nova espécie de cobra que viveu no interior de SP há milhões de anos

A remarkably preserved fossil discovered in the interior of São Paulo state is offering scientists fresh insights into the early evolution of snakes. The specimen, named Tametara mirim, lived between 85 and 75 million years ago and represents the first articulated snake fossil ever found in Brazil—meaning its bones remained connected rather than scattered, and were preserved in three dimensions. What makes this find particularly valuable is both its exceptional condition and what it reveals about ancient snake diversity. Using micro-CT scanning technology similar to three-dimensional X-rays, researchers examined the interior of the skull without damaging the fossil, digitally reconstructing the brain cavity. The analysis showed that Tametara mirim had adaptations for burrowing underground, adding an important piece to a long-standing scientific puzzle: did snakes evolve from marine ancestors or land-dwelling diggers? Rather than settling the debate, this discovery suggests a more complex picture—a mosaic where different early snake lineages already occupied varied environments from underground burrows to water. This story quietly matters because it reminds us how much we still don't know about creatures we think we understand. The fossil pushes back our knowledge of snake diversity and brain structure, showing these ancient reptiles were more varied in their lifestyles than previously imagined. For the Brazilian research team, there's personal significance too: understanding snake origins from a specimen found in their own country. With snake origins likely reaching back even further into the Jurassic period, the search continues for older, well-preserved fossils that might finally reveal how these limbless hunters first came to be.

Rivers of magma once bubbled beneath crust of Mars, research suggests

Scientists analyzing data from NASA's InSight Lander have discovered evidence that Mars once had extensive rivers of magma flowing beneath its surface—a finding that reshapes understanding of the red planet's potential to have supported life. By studying seismic waves from meteorite impacts and marsquakes, researchers at the University of Oxford identified a mysterious boundary roughly 15 miles below the Martian surface. Their analysis, published in Nature Astronomy, suggests this boundary was created by molten rock pooling deep underground and extending laterally for potentially thousands of miles. The discovery challenges earlier assumptions that Martian volcanoes were fed by simple, isolated magma chambers. Instead, Mars appears to have possessed a far more complex and interconnected subterranean plumbing system. Such a network would have been capable of creating a chemically rich crust, recycling elements, and supporting processes necessary for generating a surface atmosphere and ocean. Crucially, this system could have helped regulate the planet's climate—a function scientists previously believed required plate tectonics. This research matters because it expands the criteria for planetary habitability. Rocky planets that lack plate tectonics, once considered unlikely candidates for life, may warrant reconsideration. The finding suggests that alternative geological systems can perform similar climate-regulating and life-supporting functions, quietly broadening the universe of worlds where life might have taken hold.

Marine ornithologist Richard Caldow and his team captured the moment just as the 38-day-old chick ventured into the open behind its parent. This story matters because it demonstrates both the fragility and resilience of coastal ecosystems. While one chick won't save the colony on its own, it proves that Purbeck's puffins can still successfully reproduce under the right conditions. With motion-sensor cameras now installed to study nesting behavior and identify threats—from predators to climate change—researchers hope to unlock the puzzle of why so many eggs and chicks have been lost, and what might be done to help these iconic seabirds survive for generations to come.

A dwarf galaxy known as the Gaia Sausage—named for the distinctive sausage-shaped orbits of its stars—collided head-on with our galaxy. The collision solved a longstanding astronomical puzzle: why stars in the Milky Way's halo rotate so slowly compared to those in the galactic disc. While disc stars zip around at 220 kilometers per second, halo stars crawl at just 25 kilometers per second. The simulations revealed that this dramatic difference emerges when galaxies like ours experience head-on collisions followed by a disc flip. The Gaia Sausage itself was torn apart by the Milky Way, its stars absorbed into the halo and its mass contributing to the bulge at our galaxy's center. The Milky Way we see today—the orientation we consider normal—is actually the result of ancient chaos.
