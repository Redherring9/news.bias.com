Underwater gardening helping to restore Tasmania's lost kelp forests

Off the coast of Tasmania, a quiet revolution is taking place beneath the waves. A new month-long internship program is inviting participants from around the world to help restore the island's vanishing giant kelp forests—underwater ecosystems that have declined by an estimated 95 percent since the 1940s. Armed with laboratory-grown kelp spores wound on spools, divers brave Tasmania's frigid winter waters to hand-plant these marine forests, one strand at a time. The decline has been driven by warming ocean temperatures and invasive sea urchins that overgraze the kelp. What were once thriving underwater forests—providing habitat, food, and nursery grounds for countless marine species—disappeared in some areas in less than a year. The restoration technique, developed by researchers at the University of Tasmania, involves wrapping spore-laden spools around existing seaweed at carefully selected sites. Results have been mixed: under ideal conditions, a forest can regrow within a year, but summer heat waves, hungry fish, and storms can quickly undo the work. Scientists now emphasize the importance of choosing resilient locations and protecting what remains. This story captures something quietly hopeful—the idea that careful, hands-on work can begin to repair what's been lost. It's a reminder that restoration is as much about patience and adaptation as it is about action, and that people will travel across oceans to help tend gardens most of us will never see.

Community newspaper The Akaroa Mail celebrates 150 years

While newspapers worldwide face closures and restructuring, a small free publication on New Zealand's Banks Peninsula has reached a remarkable milestone. The Akaroa Mail, first published on July 21, 1876, continues to print 10,000 paper copies every fortnight, defying industry trends that have created what experts call "news deserts" across the country. Editor and owner Michael de Hamel has helmed the paper for 41 years, maintaining its focus on hyper-local stories and community events. His tenure has yielded surprising discoveries, including a World War Two German training glider hidden in a local basement. The paper's commitment to chronicling everyday life echoes the work of predecessor Ethel Jacobson, who ran the publication for an impressive 50 years after inheriting it from her father in 1903. Early editions documented arrivals by ship, hotel guests, and magistrates' court proceedings, alongside pages densely packed with advertisements. An exhibition at the Akaroa Museum now showcases this evolution from commercial directory to community chronicle. The Akaroa Mail's significance extends beyond nostalgia. Museum collections manager Daniel Smith notes that researchers often find fuller records of council meetings in the paper than in official documents, with crucial context about community debates. Auckland University journalism expert Greg Treadwell emphasizes the paper's value in an era when community news struggles online, calling reliable local journalism essential glue for social cohesion. For now, de Hamel continues the print tradition with support from local advertisers, content to serve a community where getting your name in the Mail still means you truly belong.

Oystercatchers, known for sharp bills and beady eyes, are making a comeback thanks to conservation

The American Oystercatcher, a distinctive shorebird with a bright orange bill and striking appearance, is experiencing a remarkable recovery along North America's East Coast. Once nearly eliminated from the region in the early 1900s due to hunting and egg collection, the species has seen its population grow by 45% since 2008—a testament to what focused conservation can achieve. The turnaround came through a coordinated effort by several nonprofit organizations that raised $10 million specifically to protect these birds. The funds supported practical measures at beach nesting sites, including restricting vehicle access and cordoning off areas during the critical breeding season. These seemingly simple interventions created safer spaces for oystercatchers to nest and raise their young, addressing one of the main threats to their survival in an era of increasing human beach use. This story offers a quiet but powerful reminder that conservation can work when communities commit resources and make space for wildlife. Peru’s only penguin species at risk from upcoming El Niño and bird flu, experts warn

Peru's Humboldt penguins, the only penguin species to breed along the country's coastal deserts, are facing a compounding crisis that has researchers deeply concerned. Between 2022 and 2025, their population plummeted by two-thirds across 34 islands and peninsulas—first struck by the devastating H5N1 avian influenza outbreak in 2022, then battered by El Niño conditions that disrupted their food supply. Now, as another strong El Niño event approaches in 2026, scientists are watching these charismatic seabirds abandon their nests in alarming numbers. At Punta San Juan de Marcona National Reserve, a critical nesting site in southern Peru, researchers documented penguin nests dropping from 190 to just 94 between April and May 2026. The culprit is warming coastal waters—up to 2°C warmer in some areas—that push the cold currents carrying Peruvian anchovies, the penguins' main food source, deeper and farther offshore. Hungry parents waiting with eggs eventually abandon their posts when fishing trips become too long and arduous. Because Humboldt penguins breed earlier than other seabirds, they serve as an early warning system; their breeding failures signal trouble ahead for the entire coastal ecosystem. Scientists note that climate change is making El Niño events more frequent and unpredictable, shrinking the recovery time between crises. This story matters because it captures a species caught at the intersection of multiple environmental pressures, where natural climate patterns amplified by human-caused warming meet emerging disease threats. The Humboldt penguin's struggle offers a window into how coastal ecosystems worldwide may fare as our climate continues to shift—a quiet but urgent reminder of interconnected vulnerabilities in the natural world.

His work extended beyond Cuban waters; recognizing that migratory turtles nest on one nation's beaches and feed in another's waters, he championed regional cooperation as Cuba's coordinator for WIDECAST, the Caribbean's sea turtle conservation network. His tagging and monitoring efforts connected local populations to the broader region, while his mentorship helped train a new generation of researchers across universities, protected areas, and community groups. This story matters because it chronicles a quiet, determined shift from exploitation to stewardship. Even as poaching and accidental capture persisted, his work documented what the law could not yet fully prevent—a reminder that conservation is measured not in single victories but in decades of patient, steadfast attention.

Decade of work helps remote island's native wildlife 'return to 1616'

On a remote island off Australia's west coast, scientists have used an unusual tool to help bring back a once-widespread native marsupial: animal droppings. Dirk Hartog Island, located about 850 kilometers north of Perth in Shark Bay World Heritage Area, is at the heart of an ambitious decade-long project called Return to 1616. The initiative aims to restore the island's ecosystem to its pre-colonial state, before Dutch explorer Dirk Hartog arrived in the early 17th century. Bettongs—small, hopping marsupials nicknamed "rat kangaroos"—were reintroduced to the island in 2025 after careful preparation. These pint-sized creatures were once common across Australia but nearly disappeared due to hunting by invasive predators like feral cats and foxes. Before scientists could safely relocate them, they needed to ensure the island could support the animals' specialized diet, which relies heavily on fungi. Researcher Rebecca Quah took on the task by analyzing bettong feces from mainland populations, extracting fungal DNA to create a molecular "fingerprint" of their dietary needs. She then compared this with soil samples from Dirk Hartog Island, which had been severely damaged by feral goats. The findings were encouraging: the island's soil contained 75 different types of fungi that could sustain the marsupials. This story offers a glimpse into the meticulous, sometimes unglamorous work behind successful conservation. It shows how modern science—from DNA analysis to ecological restoration—can help repair damage from the past and give vulnerable species a second chance in their ancestral homes.
