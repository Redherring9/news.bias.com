Norway opens new memorial for victims of 2011 Breivik massacre

Norway has unveiled a new memorial in Oslo for the 77 people killed in the country's deadliest peacetime terror attack, just days before the tragedy's 15th anniversary. On July 22, 2011, Anders Breivik detonated a car bomb in the capital before traveling to Utøya island, where he opened fire at a youth camp, killing mostly teenagers involved with the Norwegian Labour Party's youth wing. Hundreds more were injured in the attacks. The memorial, titled "Upholding," features a 12-meter-high stone mosaic of a small wading bird native to Utøya island, set within a massive blue steel frame. Artist Matias Faldbakken designed the work with dual intentions: to honor those who died and to serve as a symbol of democratic resilience against the far-right ideologies that motivated the attack. The artwork stands in Oslo's renovated government district, near the site of the 2011 bombing, with all 77 victims' names engraved on it. Survivors, relatives, Crown Prince Haakon, and government officials attended Sunday's inauguration ceremony. This memorial represents more than remembrance—it's an act of quiet defiance and civic strength. As one survivor advocate noted, the attacks targeted not just individuals but democracy itself. In opening both this memorial and a new education centre dedicated to the events, Norway continues to process one of its darkest days while affirming values of openness and resilience. The commemoration offers a moment to reflect on how communities honor loss while refusing to let terror reshape their character.

Decade of work helps remote island's native wildlife 'return to 1616'

On a remote island off Australia's west coast, scientists have used an unusual tool to help bring back a once-widespread native marsupial: animal droppings. Dirk Hartog Island, located about 850 kilometers north of Perth in Shark Bay World Heritage Area, is at the heart of an ambitious decade-long project called Return to 1616. The initiative aims to restore the island's ecosystem to its pre-colonial state, before Dutch explorer Dirk Hartog arrived in the early 17th century. Bettongs—small, hopping marsupials nicknamed "rat kangaroos"—were reintroduced to the island in 2025 after careful preparation. These pint-sized creatures were once common across Australia but nearly disappeared due to hunting by invasive predators like feral cats and foxes. Before scientists could safely relocate them, they needed to ensure the island could support the animals' specialized diet, which relies heavily on fungi. Researcher Rebecca Quah took on the task by analyzing bettong feces from mainland populations, extracting fungal DNA to create a molecular "fingerprint" of their dietary needs. She then compared this with soil samples from Dirk Hartog Island, which had been severely damaged by feral goats. The findings were encouraging: the island's soil contained 75 different types of fungi that could sustain the marsupials. This story offers a glimpse into the meticulous, sometimes unglamorous work behind successful conservation. It shows how modern science—from DNA analysis to ecological restoration—can help repair damage from the past and give vulnerable species a second chance in their ancestral homes.

Somalia races to save Radio Mogadishu’s fading archive

In a dusty, air-conditioned room in Mogadishu, thousands of reel-to-reel tapes hold fragments of Somalia's modern history. Dating back to the early 1950s, these recordings from Radio Mogadishu contain music, news bulletins, speeches, and cultural programming that once reached audiences across East Africa and the Middle East. Now, a dedicated team of archivists is racing against time to digitize roughly 400,000 hours of broadcasts before magnetic deterioration erases this irreplaceable record forever. Founded during the Italian colonial era in 1951, Radio Mogadishu grew into one of East Africa's most influential broadcasters, known for its pan-African programming reminiscent of Radio Cairo's radical voice. The station has weathered Somalia's turbulent recent history, including civil war, warlord control, and even an electrical fire in 2018 that damaged portions of the collection. During the worst fighting in 1992, a police colonel named Abshir Hashi Ali risked his life to prevent looting of the archives. Despite these challenges, UNESCO assessments found that more than 85 percent of the roughly 45,000 tapes remain playable, though deterioration continues. This preservation effort represents more than salvaging old recordings. As archivist Abdiqadir Geedi Robleh explains, the archive is "the world's largest store of Somali language music, culture, dramas and everything else," currently locked away from public access. With support from UNESCO's Memory of the World programme, Somalia aims not only to preserve these voices from the past but eventually to share them with future generations—a quiet act of cultural resilience that honors both memory and possibility.

For three decades, ecologist Kristen Messenger has been quietly cataloging reports of something that officially doesn't exist: giant earthworms in South Australia. While the internet insists no such species lives in the state, Messenger has collected dozens of eyewitness accounts—and her own photographs—of earthworms stretching up to two metres long and as thick as a garden hose, found everywhere from the Flinders Ranges to the Eyre Peninsula. The only recognized large earthworm in South Australia is the Giant Mount Lofty Earthworm, identified back in 1887 and known to inhabit the Adelaide Hills. But the new sightings come from semi-arid regions across the state, places where scientists wouldn't expect to find such creatures. Environmental consultant Geoff Dyne, who identified a new giant earthworm species near Broken Hill in 2021, believes some of these South Australian worms are "almost certainly new" species. The two-metre specimen, he says, would definitely be unprecedented. Formal recognition requires anatomical examination and publication in a scientific journal—a process that could finally put these hidden giants on the map. This story is a gentle reminder of how much remains unknown beneath our feet, even in well-studied places.
