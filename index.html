Why is it so hard for Māori and Pasifika to access weight loss drugs?

In New Zealand, a striking gap has emerged in who's using new weight-loss medications like Wegovy. Despite Māori and Pasifika communities facing significantly higher rates of obesity—45 percent for adult Māori and nearly 70 percent for Pasifika adults, compared to 33 percent nationally—these groups represent only 15 percent of prescriptions for GLP-1 drugs. The medications, which became available in New Zealand in mid-2025, cost around $400 monthly and aren't publicly funded, creating an immediate financial barrier. But doctors and patients say the disparity runs deeper than cost alone. Kayla Dove Tangira, a 29-year-old mother from Taranaki, began documenting her Wegovy journey on Instagram after noticing she hadn't seen other Māori doing so. She describes an "undeniable belief that some things are just not for us"—a sentiment shaped by economic pressure, cultural relationships with food, and systemic healthcare inequities. Dr. Sara Simmons, who works at a kaupapa-Māori clinic in Blenheim, notes that patients living in poverty often prioritize immediate needs over weight management, and limited access to nutritious food perpetuates the cycle. Meanwhile, Kim Tupene fought to secure disability funding for her prescription, only to face severe side effects that left her questioning whether she could continue. This story quietly illuminates how health inequities persist even when new treatments arrive. It's a reminder that access isn't only about availability—it's about affordability, information, cultural context, and the deeply ingrained beliefs about who healthcare innovations are truly meant to serve. Fossils reveal a prehistoric crocodile relative that walked on two legs

Paleontologists have unveiled a striking addition to the prehistoric menagerie: a two-legged, beaked, toothless creature that turns out to be a distant relative of modern crocodiles. Named Labrujasuchus expectatus, this ancient animal lived 212 million years ago during the Late Triassic period in what is now New Mexico. Its fossils were discovered at the famed Ghost Ranch site, a treasure trove of well-preserved remains. The creature's name playfully references both the ranch's old Spanish moniker—"Ranch of the Witches"—and the fact that researchers had been expecting to find such a fossil there, in contrast to an earlier shuvosaurid discovery that was so bizarre it was named "inexpectatus," or unexpected. Unlike the scaly, four-legged predators we associate with crocodiles today, L. expectatus looked radically different: it walked upright on two legs, had tiny arms, and sported a beak instead of teeth. Walking on two legs may have offered evolutionary perks like faster movement, better visibility for spotting threats or prey, and freed-up forelimbs for grasping. The landscape it inhabited was equally unlike today's desert—it was a seasonal floodplain with fluctuating rainfall, varied plant life, and occasional large wildfires. What L. expectatus ate remains uncertain, though researchers suspect it was at least omnivorous, possibly carnivorous, despite its toothless beak. It likely had to watch out for phytosaurs and other large predators of its time. This discovery reminds us that the age of dinosaurs was far more diverse than commonly imagined, with unexpected creatures experimenting with body plans that challenge our assumptions about ancient life. It's a quiet marvel of evolution's creativity.

Antarctica's first dinosaur fossil found in drawer after 40 years

Sometimes the most remarkable discoveries happen not in the field, but in the quiet corners of museum storage. A bone collected from Antarctica's James Ross Island in 1985 sat unidentified in a drawer for nearly four decades before being confirmed as the continent's first discovered dinosaur fossil. Geologist Dr. Mike Thomson had recorded it in his field notes as a "vertebra of large reptile," sketching the 10-centimeter bone with characteristic scientific precision before sending it to the British Antarctic Survey's collection in England. The fossil's true identity came to light when collections manager Dr. Mark Evans, trained in vertebrate paleontology, recognized its distinctive shape as belonging to a titanosaur—the largest land-dwelling dinosaurs, some reaching over 35 meters in length. This particular bone likely came from a relatively modest specimen, perhaps 6 or 7 meters long, living in an Antarctica unrecognizable from today's frozen landscape. About 82 million years ago, the continent was warm, heavily forested, and temperate. Scientists believe this dinosaur floated out to sea after death, eventually settling and fossilizing on the seabed. Beyond validating Dr. Thomson's careful fieldwork from 40 years prior, the discovery offers clues about how titanosaurs dispersed across Earth's southern continents. With no titanosaur fossils yet found in Australia and only limited evidence in New Zealand, this Antarctic specimen suggests these giants traveled between landmasses that were once connected. As ice continues to retreat, researchers suspect more dinosaur evidence may emerge from beneath Antarctica's frozen archive, rewriting our understanding of prehistoric life at the bottom of the world—one patient discovery at a time.
