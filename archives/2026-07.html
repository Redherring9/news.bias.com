Distress call in the Pantanal leads couple to cross the country by plane on a mission of solidarity

When a fisherman in Brazil's Pantanal wetlands suffered a toothache for 20 days with no access to dental care, dentist and pilot Caroline Lima performed an emergency extraction at a remote lodge. That single encounter inspired her and her partner Rodrigo Martins to launch "Entre Rios e Céus" (Between Rivers and Skies), a 60-day flying dental mission across five Brazilian states. Beginning in August, the couple plans to bring free oral health care to approximately 1,100 people living in isolated riverside communities. Caroline will pilot the couple's own small plane—a family aircraft purchased by her grandfather in 1973 and passed down through three generations of pilots—to reach places inaccessible by car or boat. At each stop, she'll provide preventive care, emergency procedures like extractions, and distribute hygiene kits with toothbrushes, toothpaste, and floss. The mission combines dental urgency with practical techniques suited for field conditions, including atraumatic restorative treatment. In the most remote location, Santa Maria do Boiaçu in Roraima state, the pair will stay among community members during their work. A volunteer dentist will join them at two sites with higher patient demand. What makes this story quietly remarkable is how it transforms a leisure passion—the couple's love of sport fishing—into meaningful service. Rodrigo recalls the joy on that first patient's face and realized their fishing trips could serve a greater purpose. It's a reminder that sometimes the most profound missions begin with a simple act of compassion, and that combining personal passions with community needs can lead to something much larger than initially imagined.

Djavan's songs help autistic young man communicate and lead to special meeting in Federal District

In Brasília, a young autistic man named Vittório has found his voice through an unexpected teacher: the music of Brazilian singer Djavan. According to his mother, Isaura Sarto, Vittório learned to communicate and even to read by connecting with Djavan's songs. While he attended school regularly, it was his deep interest in music that truly unlocked his ability to express himself. When he began speaking, he did so by singing along to his favorite artist's melodies, weaving music into every aspect of his communication. Last Saturday, Vittório's dedication was rewarded with a dream encounter. Before a concert celebrating Djavan's fifty-year career, the young fan met his idol backstage during sound check. The meeting moved Isaura to tears as she watched Djavan greet her son by name and interact with patience and warmth, responding naturally to Vittório's brief, direct answers and navigating the communication challenges with genuine respect. The experience has inspired Vittório to set a new goal: he wants to improve his speech clarity through continued therapy with his speech pathologist so that next time he meets Djavan, they can have a real conversation. His father notes that for the six months leading up to the concert, the prospect of this meeting became a powerful motivation. It's a quiet reminder of how art can reach across barriers, and how a meaningful connection—whether through melody or a moment of human kindness—can become the catalyst for personal growth.

England's warmest June on record down to record-breaking heatwave

England experienced its warmest June on record in 2026, with mean temperatures reaching 17.1°C—nearly 3°C above average. The heat was relentless both day and night, with frequent "tropical nights" where temperatures never dropped below 20°C. On June 26, the village of Lingwood in Norfolk recorded 37.7°C (99.9°F), shattering a June record that had stood since 1957 and surpassing even the legendary 1976 heatwave. The Met Office issued a rare red extreme heat warning, which remained in effect for an unprecedented three consecutive days in parts of eastern England. The heatwave extended well beyond Britain's borders. Across Europe, June temperature records fell in Hungary, Austria, the Netherlands, Switzerland, and Denmark. France experienced its hottest day ever recorded, with nationwide temperatures averaging over 30°C across a 24-hour period—exceeding even the deadly heatwaves of 2003 and 2019. The human cost has been sobering: France reported around 1,000 excess deaths, while Spain recorded 1,029 deaths attributable to extreme heat, many among those over 65. This story matters because it illustrates how record-breaking weather is becoming the new pattern rather than the exception. Spanish meteorologists note that half of the 12 June heatwaves recorded since 1975 have occurred in the last decade alone. While awareness and early warning systems have improved since the catastrophic 2003 heatwave that claimed tens of thousands of lives, these events remind us that extreme heat remains a quiet but serious threat—one that's becoming more frequent as the climate continues to warm.

‘We can relate’: Bosnia fans bring passion, Palestine support, to World Cup

This story offers a window into how sports can become a gathering place for scattered communities, where identity is preserved and expressed across generations. It quietly reminds us that international tournaments are never just about the game—they're about the people who carry flags, memories, and solidarity across continents, finding family in the stands.

"I arrived last but I feel like a winner": the inspiring story of Ely, the 64-year-old woman who dreamed of running a marathon and finished it in more than 7 hours

Elisabeth Osorio, a 64-year-old woman from Santiago, Chile, became an unlikely champion when she crossed the finish line of the Santiago Marathon after more than seven hours of running. Despite arthritis in her knees, limited training time due to a four-hour daily bus commute to work, and starting running only later in life, Ely completed all 42 kilometers with a single goal: to finish and earn her marathon medal. Her journey was far from easy. The hydration stations had closed by the time she reached them, and she got lost partway through the course. As she continued at her own pace, the crowds, cameras, and other 33,000 competitors had long since dispersed. The elite runners from Kenya and Ethiopia had finished hours earlier, and workers were already dismantling the event infrastructure. Yet Ely kept moving forward. When she finally approached the finish line near the presidential palace, diners at a nearby restaurant noticed her distinctive purple jersey with the Chilean flag and her name. They rushed out to cheer her on, joined by passersby who spontaneously celebrated this determined runner. Ely's story resonates beyond athletic achievement. Living in Bajos de Mena, an economically disadvantaged and stigmatized neighborhood in Puente Alto, she raised three children largely on her own and developed what she calls "bulletproof strength" through life's challenges. Her triumph offers a gentle reminder that victory isn't always about speed or rankings—sometimes it's simply about the courage to start, the resilience to continue, and the joy of reaching a goal that once seemed impossible. As Ely herself put it: "I arrived last, but I feel like a winner."

A 1,000kg mammal is wreaking havoc in Tasmania – and Neil the seal is loved for it

A one-tonne elephant seal named Neil has become an unlikely celebrity in southern Tasmania, where he returns each year to rest, socialize, and inadvertently cause cheerful chaos. The five-year-old seal—who could eventually grow to more than three times his current size—has been spotted crushing fences, blocking roads, and even leaning against parked cars. Local officials and residents have embraced his antics with affection and patience, even as they navigate the logistical challenges of sharing space with such a massive visitor. Neil's behavior, while disruptive, is entirely normal for a young elephant seal. Marine scientists explain that he's engaging in what would typically be sparring and socializing with other young males during his "haul out" periods on land. But Neil is likely alone—possibly the descendant of an inexperienced seal who wandered to Tasmania by mistake. Without peers to tussle with, he practices his fighting skills on bollards and traffic cones instead. When he sleeps pressed against fences, experts believe he's seeking the comfort of contact that seals usually enjoy in crowded colonies. His solitary visits highlight both his resilience and his isolation. Beyond Neil's individual story, elephant seals face serious threats. The species was recently upgraded to "vulnerable" after avian flu devastated major populations, killing more than 90% of pups in some colonies. Neil's annual return offers a moment of connection to a species under pressure, reminding Tasmanians of the wildlife that shares their coastline. His charm lies not just in his size or mischief, but in what he represents: a solitary creature seeking companionship, and a community willing to make room for him.

Beavers stop station from flooding, ecologists say

A family of beavers living in west London has done something remarkable: they've solved a flooding problem that had plagued a nearby railway station for years. Since the mammals were reintroduced to Paradise Fields in Ealing in 2023, Greenford station downstream has stopped flooding after heavy rainfall—a problem that once occurred regularly. The beavers, often called nature's engineers, have transformed the landscape by building dams and creating complex wetlands that slow and absorb water during downpours. What once took minutes to rush through the system now takes hours to percolate through the beavers' network of dams, easing pressure on the drainage infrastructure. According to conservationist Sean McCormack, the flooding has completely stopped in the two winters since the beavers established themselves. The ecological benefits extend beyond flood control: the site has seen an explosion of invertebrates, new bird species, and at least two additional bat species, breathing life into what was once a neglected area. This story offers a gentle reminder of how working with nature, rather than against it, can yield elegant solutions to modern problems. The recent birth of at least two beaver kits has local residents gathering at dusk with cameras, hoping to catch a glimpse of the family. It's a tale that combines practical infrastructure benefits with the simple joy of witnessing wildlife return to a place where it was hunted to extinction centuries ago—proof that sometimes the best engineers wear fur.

Oceans reached record temperature at the end of June, European observatory reports

The world's oceans reached their highest recorded temperatures for late June, according to data from Europe's Copernicus observatory. On June 21st, two independent analyses confirmed that sea surface temperatures surpassed the records set on the same date in both 2023 and 2024, with readings reaching between 20.86°C and 21°C—roughly 0.1°C above previous peaks. The measurements cover ocean areas outside polar regions, between 60 degrees north and south latitude. The record arrives alongside the emergence of a new El Niño event, a natural climate phenomenon characterized by warmer-than-normal waters in the equatorial Pacific that influences rainfall and temperature patterns worldwide. Copernicus forecasts suggest this El Niño could reach an intensity not observed in decades. The observatory also points to unusually intense heat in the northern Pacific Ocean as a contributing factor. Director Carlo Buontempo noted that current conditions may signal "the beginning of a new phase, leading again into unknown territory," with more temperature records likely in coming months. This story matters because warming oceans don't stay contained—they reshape weather systems, fuel extreme storms, accelerate sea level rise, and threaten marine ecosystems through coral bleaching and shifting species distributions. Marine biologist Alicia Pérez-Porro emphasizes that tracking these changes is essential for understanding their short and long-term effects on communities and climate. It's a quiet alarm bell about interconnected systems, reminding us that the ocean's rising fever touches shores far beyond where the measurements are taken.

Why is it so hard for Māori and Pasifika to access weight loss drugs?

In New Zealand, a striking gap has emerged in who's using new weight-loss medications like Wegovy. Despite Māori and Pasifika communities facing significantly higher rates of obesity—45 percent for adult Māori and nearly 70 percent for Pasifika adults, compared to 33 percent nationally—these groups represent only 15 percent of prescriptions for GLP-1 drugs. The medications, which became available in New Zealand in mid-2025, cost around $400 monthly and aren't publicly funded, creating an immediate financial barrier. But doctors and patients say the disparity runs deeper than cost alone. Kayla Dove Tangira, a 29-year-old mother from Taranaki, began documenting her Wegovy journey on Instagram after noticing she hadn't seen other Māori doing so. She describes an "undeniable belief that some things are just not for us"—a sentiment shaped by economic pressure, cultural relationships with food, and systemic healthcare inequities. Dr. Sara Simmons, who works at a kaupapa-Māori clinic in Blenheim, notes that patients living in poverty often prioritize immediate needs over weight management, and limited access to nutritious food perpetuates the cycle. Meanwhile, Kim Tupene fought to secure disability funding for her prescription, only to face severe side effects that left her questioning whether she could continue. This story quietly illuminates how health inequities persist even when new treatments arrive. It's a reminder that access isn't only about availability—it's about affordability, information, cultural context, and the deeply ingrained beliefs about who healthcare innovations are truly meant to serve. The voices here invite reflection on what equity really means when breakthrough treatments enter the market.

Bumba Meu Boi: Carried in his grandfather's arms since infancy, 4-year-old boy continues three-generation tradition in Piauí

On Brazil's National Bumba Meu Boi Day, a four-year-old boy named José Rayan is carrying forward a cherished family tradition that spans three generations. Since he was six months old, José has been attending rehearsals and performances of the Bumba Meu Boi Imperador da Ilha, a cultural group in Teresina, Piauí. His grandfather, Dom Riba, recalls carrying the infant to meetings and shows, and now the boy participates as a vaqueiro, or cowboy character, fulfilling a promise his mother made to São João. Bumba Meu Boi is a vibrant folk tradition combining dance, music, and theater that tells the story of a resurrected ox. The Imperador da Ilha group has been performing since 1934, making it 92 years old and a pillar of Piauí's popular culture. Dom Riba himself joined the tradition at age twelve and later became the "miolo," the person who animates the ox costume from within. The group has passed through multiple generations of the Araújo family, currently led by Raimundo Araújo and his son Fábio, keeping the cultural legacy alive through dedicated teaching and community involvement. This story offers a touching glimpse into how cultural traditions endure through families who view preservation as both heritage and mission. Beyond performances, the group serves its community by hosting capoeira classes, Alcoholics Anonymous meetings, and health services for the elderly. It's a quiet reminder that folklore isn't frozen in the past—it lives, breathes, and grows through the hands that lovingly pass it forward, from grandfather to grandson, one dance step at a time.
