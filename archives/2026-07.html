Human DNA at least 2,000 years old found on cave walls

Scientists have made a remarkable discovery in caves across Spain and Portugal: human DNA preserved in rock walls for at least 2,000 years. The finding, published in Nature, reveals that cave surfaces can hold genetic material far longer than previously demonstrated, opening new windows into how ancient people interacted with these underground spaces. The research began as part of the FIRST ART project, which originally aimed to chemically date rock art and pigments in Spain's Maltravieso Cave, home to some of Europe's oldest paintings. Researchers expanded their scope to test whether DNA could be recovered directly from cave paintings—a departure from relying solely on traditional sources like bones or tools. The team analyzed 24 rock art panels across 11 caves, including simple marks, negative hand images, and pigment fragments. Using modern extraction and sequencing techniques, they examined both pigmented and unpigmented wall sections, sediments, bones, and prehistoric painting tools. The biggest surprise came from Spain's Covarón Cave, where biological traces appeared on control samples—walls where no genetic material was expected. While researchers cannot definitively link the DNA to the artists themselves, the discovery demonstrates that cave walls can preserve human biological traces for millennia. Some of the studied sites had entrances sealed by rockfalls 4,000 years ago, suggesting the genetic material could be even older than the confirmed 2,000-year minimum age. This finding offers archaeologists a new tool for understanding how ancient populations used caves and where they left their marks—not just in pigment and stone, but in the invisible threads of their genetic legacy.

British swallowtail split from European cousins much earlier than thought, study finds

A new genetic study has revealed that Britain's endangered swallowtail butterfly is far more ancient and distinctive than previously believed. The British swallowtail, found only in the Norfolk Broads wetlands, was long thought to have developed its unique characteristics—smaller size, darker coloring—during the 8,000 years since rising seas isolated it from continental populations. Instead, whole-genome sequencing shows it split from European cousins between 200,000 and 1.7 million years ago, making it a wetland specialist that likely once ranged across northern Europe's marshes. This discovery arrives at a crucial moment for conservation strategy. Some experts had proposed introducing the more common continental swallowtail to Britain, potentially allowing it to hybridize with the rarer British subspecies. The continental variety thrives on multiple food plants, while British swallowtail caterpillars depend almost exclusively on milk parsley, a rare wetland plant. Both butterfly and plant now face twin threats: rising sea levels are salinating the freshwater Norfolk Broads, while warming temperatures are already bringing continental swallowtails to southern England during warm summers. Most British breeding populations live at or below sea level, making them especially vulnerable. What makes this story quietly remarkable is how it reframes conservation priorities through the lens of deep history. Rather than viewing the British swallowtail as a recent, fragile variant, scientists now recognize it as a unique evolutionary lineage and the last remnant of a once-widespread wetland population. Conservationists are now planning to establish new populations in protected wetlands across England, from Suffolk to Somerset to Yorkshire—preserving not just a rare butterfly, but a living connection to Europe's ancient wetland past.
